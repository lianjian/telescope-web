What is Telescope?

Telescope is a machine learning assisted toolkit for image and video compositors with applications in visual effects, matte painting and diverse use cases across the video post production pipeline. Tools from existing compositing packages can interact with our novel ML core to assist or completely automate the rotoscoping process. The core of our rotoscoping program is the deep learning model that takes the image to be cropped and an associated trimap, and outputs the cropped portion. The image to be cropped can be of anything the user wishes. This is a new and essential feature, as previous ML solutions use algorithms that must be told what they are isolating (e.g. a person, car, building, etc.). All Telescope needs is a rough visual of what it should cut out, and our model takes it from there.

Rotoscoping & Image Matting

Rotoscoping is the process of frame by frame selecting and isolating a given feature in a video, such that you can produce a video clip of exclusively that feature on a transparent background. This allows the isolated subject to be composited into another video clip with a different background; an example of this effect in action is in the movie Forrest Gump where he is inserted into historical footage as a recipient of a Medal of Honor, given to him by President LBJ. A feature length film will often consist of hundreds of rotoscoped shots with multiple tracked mattes per image, and this is the primary job of thousands of roto artists accross the world.

Typical roto tasks include: selecting and tracking objects like on-set wires and rigs to remove them from the movie, and isolating objects for compositing - which is how effects artists create 3D interactions; by layering many flat, 2D clips of the same subject. The Dinosaurs in Jurassic Park are all clips rendered out by other graphics software and inserted into the film footage. Talented artists painstakingly used rotoscoping on the actors and many objects in the scene, so the Dinosaurs could appear to pass behind and around them in the massively multi-layered clips. Rotoscoping can be an incredibly challenging and time-consuming process, as the artist must adjust their selection, or mask for every frame to match the new position and movement of the subject.

Trimaps

A trimap is a single channel image with three regions: white, which indicates the definite foreground of the feature to be extracted; black, which indicates the background to be excluded from the extraction; and gray, which indicates the uncertain regions that need to be processed, such as the edges of the subject, or complex regions like hair.

Telescope works by taking two inputs: the RGB source image and the single channel trimap and processes them through a nueral network to output a single channel alpha matte, which is used in compositing software to extract the feature from the RGB source image.
